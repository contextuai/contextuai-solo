# SWOT Analysis

A comprehensive SWOT Analysis framework for evaluating an organization's strategic position by examining internal strengths and weaknesses alongside external opportunities and threats. This blueprint guides teams through a structured assessment and produces actionable strategies by cross-referencing all four quadrants.

## Objective

Complete a thorough SWOT analysis that identifies the most significant internal and external factors affecting the organization, then derive concrete action items from the cross-analysis of these factors.

## Steps

### 1. Identify Strengths
Catalog the internal advantages your organization holds over competitors. Consider proprietary technology, brand reputation, team expertise, financial resources, customer loyalty, operational efficiency, and unique partnerships. For each strength, rate its significance (high/medium/low) and assess whether it is sustainable or at risk of erosion. Gather input from multiple departments to avoid blind spots.

### 2. Identify Weaknesses
Honestly assess internal areas where your organization underperforms or lacks capability. Examine gaps in talent, outdated processes, technical debt, limited market reach, funding constraints, and customer complaints. For each weakness, determine whether it is fixable in the short term or represents a structural limitation. Prioritize weaknesses that directly impact your ability to compete.

### 3. Identify Opportunities
Scan the external environment for favorable conditions you can exploit. Look at market trends, regulatory changes, emerging technologies, competitor vulnerabilities, demographic shifts, and unmet customer needs. For each opportunity, estimate the window of time available to act and the resources required to capture it. Distinguish between opportunities that align with your strengths and those that would require new capabilities.

### 4. Identify Threats
Evaluate external factors that could harm your organization's performance. Consider new competitors, changing customer preferences, economic downturns, supply chain disruptions, regulatory tightening, and technological obsolescence. For each threat, assess its likelihood and potential impact. Identify which threats are already materializing versus those that are still on the horizon.

### 5. Conduct Cross-Analysis
Map the intersections between all four quadrants to generate strategic options. Strength-Opportunity (SO) strategies leverage what you do well to capture opportunities. Weakness-Opportunity (WO) strategies address gaps so you can pursue new openings. Strength-Threat (ST) strategies use your advantages to defend against threats. Weakness-Threat (WT) strategies identify vulnerabilities that need urgent attention to prevent damage.

### 6. Prioritize and Assign Action Items
From the cross-analysis, select the top 5-7 strategic initiatives. For each initiative, define a clear owner, a timeline, required resources, and measurable success criteria. Categorize actions as immediate (next 30 days), short-term (1-3 months), or medium-term (3-12 months). Ensure there is a balance between offensive moves (capturing opportunities) and defensive moves (mitigating threats).

## Expected Output

A completed SWOT matrix with rated factors in each quadrant, a cross-analysis table showing SO/WO/ST/WT strategies, and a prioritized action plan with owners, timelines, and success metrics.

## Recommended Agents

- **CEO** — Provides the strategic vision and ensures the analysis aligns with the company's direction.
- **COO** — Assesses operational strengths and weaknesses and owns process-related action items.
- **Market Researcher** — Supplies external data on opportunities and threats including competitor and market intelligence.
